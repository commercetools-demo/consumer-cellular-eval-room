Where the Transformation Stands

What's Done. What's Left. What CT Covers.

Three years in, Carparts.com has moved fast. CMS, AI search, product recommendations, catalog services, and checkout - all modernized and running on AWS. CarParts+ loyalty is going live. The remaining gap is smaller in surface area but carries the highest business impact: the customer and personalization layer, the piece that ties everything else together and was the reason the e-commerce engine modernization stalled. Here's our read of what that gap is costing.

Five Gaps That Remain
01
Every visitor gets the same experience - DIYer or professional, first-time or loyal.
Today there is no preference capture, no segmentation, and no signal feeding back from customer identity into the browse experience. A professional mechanic and a first-time DIYer land on the same homepage, see the same search results, and get the same product ranking. The data to differentiate them doesn't flow into a unified profile that drives the experience in real time.
90% new visitors · 10% returning
02
Customer acquisition cost is rising as paid search ROI declines.
AI-driven search is changing how customers discover parts. Spending on Google to acquire a customer who leaves and never comes back is a model under pressure. The lever is retention: converting that 90% first-time visitor into a returning customer who buys directly. That requires knowing who the customer is, capturing their preferences, and using that data to make the next visit better than the last.
Goal: 50/50 new vs. returning
03
CarParts+ loyalty is live - but customer data isn't connected to the browse layer.
Loyalty state, tier, and points balance are available. But without a unified customer attribute model, loyalty state can't feed back into browse, product ranking, banners, or pricing in real time. The infrastructure exists; the connective tissue between customer profile and experience doesn't. commercetools Customer Groups and extensible Custom Types are exactly that connective tissue.
Loyalty live · personalization not yet
04
B2B wholesale is 10% of business on a legacy stack - with plans to grow.
CarParts Wholesale runs on the same legacy systems being modernized on the B2C side. Plans to grow the wholesale business are constrained by that legacy. commercetools handles B2C and B2B under one platform and one license - the same engineering team, the same APIs, the same customer data model - with native B2B primitives (account hierarchy, dealer pricing, quote-to-order) that don't require a separate build.
B2C + B2B · one platform
05
The e-commerce engine goal was never just cart and checkout.
The reason the prior modernization effort stalled wasn't the checkout layer - that's running on AWS. It was that the full goal included customer + personalization feeding back into the browse experience, and a complete solution couldn't be scoped cleanly. commercetools' modular adoption model answers that directly: adopt customer management and segmentation first, connect it to your existing AI search and checkout layer, and extend from there on a timeline Carparts.com controls.

Flexibility: Extend the Platform, Don't Fork It

The concern we hear most from teams on custom engines: "A SaaS platform can't be flexible enough for what we do." The commercetools answer is API Extensions - synchronous hooks that fire at any commerce event and return custom logic inline. Your frontend connects via REST or GraphQL. Your business logic runs in your infrastructure. The platform is the backbone, not the ceiling.

The Scenario
Carparts.com's product team wants to launch a tiered loyalty program: customers earn points per order, points unlock a pricing tier, and a new checkout step lets them apply points as partial payment. In the current custom engine, this is a 5–8 week sprint across catalog, cart, checkout, and payments - all on commodity infrastructure. The question: how does this work in commercetools, and how much of it is configuration vs. custom code?
Tailored Questions - Flexibility

API Extensions are synchronous webhooks that fire at designated commerce lifecycle events: before or after a cart update, before order creation, on customer authentication, on price calculation, and more. Your extension receives the full event payload, runs custom logic in your infrastructure, and returns a modified response - which the platform applies inline before returning to the caller.

For the loyalty scenario: when a customer adds a product to cart, an API Extension fires. Your loyalty service checks the customer's point balance and tier, applies the appropriate price override via a Direct Discount object, and returns the enriched cart. The platform accepts this as a valid cart update. No platform modification, no fork, no platform release cycle dependency.

Extensions are configured in Merchant Center or via the Extensions API. They point to any HTTPS endpoint - a Lambda, a Cloud Run function, or a service in your existing infrastructure. Carparts.com owns the logic; commercetools provides the hook.

commercetools is fully headless. The platform exposes REST and GraphQL APIs. There is no bundled frontend, no required template, no storefront dependency. Your existing frontend - whatever stack it runs on - queries the commercetools APIs directly. Cart, checkout, search, product display, customer authentication: all driven through APIs your frontend calls.

In practice, the most common migration pattern is replacing the commerce backend incrementally. The Carparts.com frontend continues to serve traffic; the API calls that previously hit your custom engine are rerouted to commercetools endpoints one flow at a time. The customer sees no change. Your engineers flip one integration at a time and validate before moving to the next.

commercetools also provides a Frontend SDK and Composable Commerce Starter for teams that want to accelerate frontend development - but it is never required.

Your fitment intelligence lives in the search layer you control; commercetools holds the commerce record.

Configuration handles the model; your code handles only the proprietary calculation.

Pre-warming, capacity planning, SRE on-call during promotions, post-incident reviews - these are real costs that compound as the business grows. commercetools is versionless, multi-region SaaS with Platform Insights for self-serve observability. Your team's job during a traffic spike is to watch the business metrics, not the infrastructure.

The platform has processed billions of orders across its customer base, including Black Friday and Cyber Monday events for large global retailers. The traffic spike that would be an emergency on a custom-built single-tenant infrastructure is a routine scaling event for the platform. Carparts.com's ops concern shifts from "will the commerce layer survive?" to "is our frontend and CDN handling the load?" - which is a different and smaller surface area to manage.

Customer & Personalization: The Missing Layer

The most direct path to reducing customer acquisition cost and growing returning visitor rates is knowing who the customer is and using that to shape the experience. Today, everyone who lands on Carparts.com gets the same browse experience - DIYer or professional, loyalty member or not, first car or fleet. commercetools' customer data model, Customer Groups, and extensible Custom Types are the architecture to capture, store, and act on that signal.

The Scenario
A customer creates an account on Carparts.com. During onboarding, they indicate they're a professional mechanic with a 2-bay shop, own 3 vehicles, and are interested in brakes and oil. They join CarParts+. Their next session: the browse experience reflects their profile - pro-grade parts surface first, their loyalty balance is visible in the header, banners are tuned to their vehicle makes, and they're recognized as a returning customer rather than an anonymous visitor. How does this work in commercetools - and what does Carparts.com configure vs. build?
Tailored Questions - Customer & Personalization

commercetools Customer objects have a set of native fields (email, name, addresses, date of birth, customer number) and an extensible Custom Type layer for any attribute your business defines. DIYer/professional classification, vehicle ownership, preferred categories, CarParts+ tier, and preference flags are all modeled as Custom Fields on the Customer object - defined once in Merchant Center, populated at registration or via API, and immediately available on every subsequent API response.

There is no schema migration or database change required to add a new attribute. You define the Custom Type, add the field, and start writing to it. The attribute is available in the Customer API response for every downstream consumer - your personalization service, your search ranking layer, your frontend banner logic - immediately.

Customer Groups are a native first-class concept in commercetools. A customer belongs to one Customer Group at a time, and that group membership is evaluated at every commerce event: price lookup, cart calculation, product visibility, promotion eligibility. When a customer's loyalty tier advances from Bronze to Gold, you update their Customer Group assignment via the API - the next request they make immediately reflects Gold-tier pricing, Gold-tier promotional eligibility, and any other group-scoped configuration.

For Carparts.com's use case: a DIYer Customer Group and a Professional Customer Group carry different default price tiers (professional accounts often get trade pricing), different product ranking signals fed to your search layer, and different promotional eligibility. Group assignment happens at account creation based on onboarding answers, and can be updated automatically when behavior signals warrant a reclassification - via an API Extension hooked to order placement or a Subscription event.

Tailored Questions - B2B Commerce

commercetools Business Units are the native B2B account model. A Business Unit represents a company (a repair shop, a fleet operator, a dealership). Individual buyers within that company are Customer objects associated with the Business Unit. The Business Unit carries the account-level configuration: credit limit, payment terms, approved product catalog, and discount tier.

For CarParts Wholesale's use case - independent shops buying on trade accounts - this maps directly: each shop is a Business Unit, each buyer at the shop is a Customer associated with that Unit, and account-level pricing flows from the Business Unit's Customer Group assignment.
